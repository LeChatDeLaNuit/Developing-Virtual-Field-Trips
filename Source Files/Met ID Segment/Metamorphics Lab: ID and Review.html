LAB 6: METAMORPHIC ROCKS & TECTONIC SETTINGS

Objectives

When you have completed this lab, you will be able to:

  1. Learn how and where the common metamorphic rocks are formed
  2. Understand how rock properties provide insight into their formation and history
  3. Evaluate the properties of 8 common metamorphic rocks to determine their identity
  4. Describe different tectonic settings utilizing plate motions rock types and the relationships between them

Additional Info (Sample B)
  • Appearance: Alternating layers of light colored and dark colored minerals. Mostly white and black bands, with some pink grains throughout.
  • Density: Moderately dense.
  • Reaction with acid: None
  • Scratches glass
Hint (Sample B)
  • There appears to be a variety of different colored bands within this sample (white, black, and some pink specks.) A rock from earlier on also had minerals matching these colors–perhaps they’re related?
Sample C

Additional Info (Sample C)
  • Appearance: Thin, dark layers within the rock. Very fine grained crystals. Mostly flat (can see thin sheets where broken). Has a matte finish. Decent “ping” sound when struck.
  • Density: Moderately dense.
  • Reaction with acid: None
  • Does not scratch glass
Hint (Sample C)
  • This rock is extremely similar to its protolith. There may be a sedimentary rock that matches this description.
Sample D

Additional Info (Sample D)
  • Appearance: Medium to large crystals, with no distinct layering. Somewhat clear crystals; mostly all one color.
  • Density: Moderately dense.
  • Reaction with acid: None
  • Scratches glass
Hint (Sample D)
  • With no distinct layers, this rock must be nonfoliated. The other information should help narrow it down to one ID.
Sample E

Additional Info (Sample E)
  • Appearance: Layering present within the rock, with flat crystals present at outermost surfaces. Pieces and flakes of biotite form glitter-like scales.
  • Density: Moderately dense.
  • Reaction with acid: None
  • Does not scratch glass
Hint (Sample E)
  • Biotite is part of the mica family of minerals.
Sample F

Additional Info (Sample F)
  • Appearance: Dark grey, translucent crystals. No visible layers.
  • Density: Moderately dense.
  • Reaction with acid: Vigorous reaction with many bubbles.
  • Does not scratch glass
Hint (Sample F)
  • Both the protolith of this rock and its primary mineral also react with hydrochloric acid.
Sample G

Additional Info (Sample G)
  • Appearance: Thin layers within the rock. Slight grey-green color. Undulating surface with a shiny appearance, similar to graphite.
  • Density: Moderately dense.
  • Reaction with acid: None
  • Does not scratch glass
Hint (Sample G)
  • This rock has undergone enough metamorphism for minerals to start realigning, but not enough for them to form large crystals.
